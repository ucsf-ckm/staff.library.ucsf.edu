Website Redesign: Getting a Handle on Our Information Architecture

August 12, 2016July 7, 2016 by Tab McDaniel

We’ve already posted a few times about evaluating and refining our content for maximum scannability (on the web, scanning is the new reading). Today I want to share how the web team is working to better organize and structure our content for maximum findability. In other words, we need to get a grip on our information architecture (IA).

Information Architecture? What’s That?

 

Putting together the spreadsheet for the content audit crystalized something we’ve all known for some time: there is way too much stuff on the library website. Some of the information is out of date, some of the information is redundant, and some of it is unfortunately mislabeled: we’re using language in our menus and page headings that our patrons just don’t understand.

The result is a bloated site with some useful content that is too hard to find. User testing, anecdotal evidence, probably some of your own experiences, and feedback from both LibQUAL 2013 and LibQUAL 2015 tell us that our patrons have too hard a time finding what they need on our website.

We had some ideas about how to fix our findability problem …

  • organizing and grouping our content in a way that makes sense to our patrons
  • using language our patrons use to label key areas and attract their notice
  • streamlining the site by eliminating redundant and out-of-date content

… but as always we want to test our assumptions.

How do you test an information architecture?

We used a web-based tool from OptimalWorkshop to build a tree test: a text-only outline of our IA.

 

137 participants, mostly from UCSF and mostly moderate-to-heavy users of our site, tried to accomplish 9 discrete tasks designed to test:

  • practical considerations like
    • do users expect to find Library Hours under Use the Library or About the Library?
  • thornier problems like anything to do with full-text access
    • for both on-campus and off-campus problems, where would most users expect to find help?
  • findability problems around our services
    • if someone needs help with using EndNote, or wants to attend a PubMed workshop, where would they look?

The tasks were designed to test needs/problems of a wide range of our users, written from the points of view of the library user personas we created after extensive conversations with public-facing library staffers, in-person user interviews and user tests, and – of course – feedback gleaned from LibQUAL 2013 and 2015. As I mentioned in a post last Fall:

Keeping Researcher Jeff, Faculty Nancy, Nursing Student Kofi, Clinician Evelyn, and Archives Alan in mind throughout our design processes will ensure we’re always aligned with our user’s needs, and not designing to our own biases.

 

We’ve updated our menus and our site-wide information architecture based on some obvious (and some surprising) lessons learned from the IA test.

This is why we test: logical assumptions don’t always line up with user behavior

For all the tasks where we thought the Help menu would attract clicks, like help me with EndNote, help me write better PubMed queries, or help me get the full-text, Help was not the preferred path. The following tests where users expect to find our classes and workshops. Help is the only route to our Classes/Workshops information, but it was the 4th most popular choice.

The key takeaway is to put ‘helpful’ information right in the users path by incorporating it into the content, rather than expect them to seek out a HELP menu or button.

We’re all very aware that problems accessing full-text and UCSF-authenticated resources, both on-campus and off-campus, are a major pain-point for our users. On the current site, troubleshooting off-campus access is buried a few levels down in the information architecture. The language we use elsewhere on the site and in our navigation, “Off-Campus Access” seems informational rather than problem-solving. This is information our patrons seek out when they’re in trouble, not when everything is working as expected.

We thought the solution might be to bring this key issue up a few levels and group it with other technical and access support content. We created a Tech Support menu item, assuming that it was a transparent term everyone would understand and be attracted to.

Boy, were we wrong. We asked 2 questions around full-text access issues: one where the user is on-campus and on UCSFwpa, the other where the user is off-campus. In both instances, most users were attracted to Use the Library. Not Tech Support. Not Help.

In fact, for nearly every task where Tech Support made sense to us … it simply did not to our users.

Takeaways and Next Steps

We’ve made some changes to how we organize our content, and to our main menus, based on what we learned from the IA test:

  • The fact that most users sought help under Use the Library for both on-network and off-campus full-text access issues was a lightbulb moment for us. On our current site, we talk about on-network troubleshooting in a UC-eLinks Quick Guide, and we talk about off-campus access problems somewhere else entirely. It would seem that for our users, problems with full-text are problems with full-text, regardless of where the user is. We’re working on a combined page with a more transparent heading — Full-text Access Problems? — where the content will be grouped by On-Campus and Off-Campus subheads. We hope this will prove more useful; further testing will let us know.
  • We went back to the drawing board for our tech-specific content. Instead of Tech Support, we’re calling the new menu item Tech Resources. Future user tests will reveal if this labeling works, and if a top-level Tech menu is attractive to our patrons.
  • We respected the data and added Full-text Access Problems? and Classes & Workshops to an updated Use the Library menu.
  • We’re also adding a My Borrowing top-level menu to our navigation. This change comes from earlier user tests around account finding and item renewal issues. In nearly every case, users kept looking for a login or my account link in the top-right hand of the screen. My Stuff in the upper right has become a common enough web convention that we should be meeting user expectations.
  • We’re experimenting with removing the Research Support and Course Support menu items. The web team uses a visual analytics service, Crazy Egg, that creates heat maps of user clicks on any given page. We’ve periodically used the service to check menu click activity since the current menus were introduced in June 2014, and unfortunately Research Support and Course Support get very little action. We know that important content is linked from here, but our users aren’t looking for it here. We’re working with library staffers to figure out how to attract user attention to these vital resources directly on the home page, and embedded into other content, since the menus just aren’t working.
Proposed New Menus
New, Persistent Helper Menu

We’re also testing our idea of putting key content right in the user’s path with a new, persistent helper menu that will live on the right-hand side of every page.

 

As always, we’ll test out these ideas with our patrons, and keep iterating on design decisions that fail. 

All the Data, All the Analysis

If you’d like to see all 9 tasks and complete analysis from this IA test, including some very nifty data visualizations, you can access the full report at Optimal Workshop.

We’d Love Your Help

The library personas we turn to when trying to solve problems from our patrons’ perspective are a living document to be refined (and expanded) as we learn more and/or try to solve narrower problems. If you think we’ve missed a key perspective or user pain-point, we’d love to hear from you.

We also need your input on the redesign in general: both on the proposed changes to our IA and menu structure, and as we collaborate with library departments on their content. We hope to have a staging server up soon, so that staff can play around with, and weigh in on, the new site before it goes live.

We’ll keep posting to the blog with updates, opportunities to participate, and requests for feedback. We also have a new #website channel in our UCSF Library Slack where we can talk all things redesign.

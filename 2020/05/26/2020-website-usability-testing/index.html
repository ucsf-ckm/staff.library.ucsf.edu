2020 Website Usability Testing

May 26, 2020 by Anna VanderJagt

In a sense, the website is the largest branch of the UCSF Library. More users interact with the library through our website than through our physical spaces, and this has been especially true during COVID-19 closures. Part of maintaining the quality of the website involves gathering user data and running periodic usability tests to uncover any problems users may have in connecting to the library materials and services that they need to do their work.

The last comprehensive website usability testing was done in 2016. As was noted at the time, a lot of the expected findings didn’t pan out. In some cases our assumptions of what users needed were right, and in others they were wrong. As was written in the summary for the 2016 testing, “This is why we test: logical assumptions don’t always line up with user behavior.”

After usage pattern analysis, persona analysis, and a website audit in 2018–2019, webpage inconsistencies and knowledge gaps (e.g., the Help Center’s integration into the main website) were revealed. We restructured the main website’s architecture, added Ask an Expert to the main menu, and worked with library staff to redesign various webpages to improve discoverability and findability of UCSF Library expertise and services.

Examples of redesigned webpages:

  • Data Science (see blog post)
  • Makers Lab (see blog post)
  • Student IT Support (see blog post)
  • Scholarly Communication (see blog post)

Now it’s time to test changes with users to ensure the website easily facilitates connection to materials, services, and expert help. Our goal is to gather data from a diverse group of UCSF affiliates and evaluate the site structure from their perspectives.

Questions we’re trying to answer

We assembled a list of questions based on what’s changed with the website and questions that we ourselves had. Staff input on the questions below is needed. Are there particular areas of the website that you’d like to see tested? Let us know!

  • What are the users’ pain points and roadblocks in finding services and people to help them?
    • We moved “Ask an Expert” to the main navigation last year. Do people avoid clicking “Ask an Expert” right away?
  • Are there any issues around the design of the user interface?
    • Do people scroll down pages to get to relevant sections? Or do they leave if they don’t see what they need “above the fold”?
  • Can people easily locate library collection materials, such as Archives collections?
  • Can people easily find Archives and Special Collections services?
  • Can people easily locate their library and/or UCSF account information?
  • Do people waffle between “Use the Library” and “Ask an Expert” sections of the website?
    • Do people know what “Library Tech Commons” means or entails?
    • Do users know that they can find specialized help from people via “Searching and Managing the Literature”?
    • Do users know what “Faculty Support” means or entails?
    • Do users know what “Scholarly Communications” means or entails?
    • Do users know what to expect from “Multimedia Hardware and Software”?
    • Do users know what “Data Science” means or entails? 
  • Can people reserve the equipment they need, like Makers Lab equipment?
  • Are users able to easily locate specific library events/workshops and register, like finding when the next programming workshop is?
  • Can users easily locate specialized library spaces to reserve, like the eLearning Studio?
  • Can users find a specific space, like Tech Commons, and locate hours and location?
  • Can users easily navigate to specific equipment documentation, like video cameras, when evaluating equipment to reserve?
  • Can users navigate to specific service areas and book consultations?
  • Can users find tools, information, or documentation for specific areas like bioinformatics or CLE?
    • Can people find the Help Center through specific service pages and the homepage?
    • Can people find curated lists of tools and resources for their specific needs on service pages?
  • Are users easily finding relevant posts like recent scholarly publishing news?
  • Are users finding how to sign up for newsletters whether that’s through the footer or specific pages like Instructional Design?

Testing methods

We’ll be using a few user testing methods to analyze and measure the website’s usability including remote moderated open card sorting, tree testing, and usability task interviews.

Open card sort: Test participants will sort cards into categories that make sense to them, and label each category themselves. This exercise helps us understand users’ mental models and how to organize library content.

Tree testing: Test participants evaluate a hierarchical category structure of the library website, or tree, by identifying the locations in the tree where specific tasks can be completed. This exercise shows us how easily people find information using the existing menu labels “Find Materials”, “Use the Library”, “Ask an Expert”, and “My Account.”

Usability task interviews: Test participants are provided with a scenario and then asked to use the library website to accomplish a task related to the scenario. This exercise helps us identify usability issues with both site architecture and webpage design.

To determine test session duration expectations, four library staff members participated in test sessions. (Thanks, Jenny, Josephine, Scott, and Rebecca!) Sessions took approximately 30 minutes to complete. During test sessions, some patterns were revealed and edits were made to the task questions to get the most out of end-user testing.

Testing will be split into two phases to keep sessions under 30 minutes and take place via Zoom during June and July.

We’d love your help

Along with staff feedback on questions to answer during usability testing, we also need help reaching a broad, diverse audience of testers. If you know of UCSF affiliates (e.g., faculty, staff, postdocs, students, etc.) who would be willing to participate, send them a link to complete the screening survey (http://tiny.ucsf.edu/Wb6Ncd) or connect us via email.

The ARL Leadership Institute – or how Ariel and Sarah learned about Learning Organizations

March 11, 2019March 11, 2019 by Ariel Deardorff

In the absence of meeting reports, we (Sarah and Ariel) wanted to share a quick update on an interesting leadership institute we participated in last month.

The Institute

On February 19-21st, we joined 30 other UC Librarians at UC Berkeley for an ARL Leadership and Management Institute focused on building Learning Organizations. In case you are not familiar with this term (it was new to us), a learning organization is “an organization skilled at creating, acquiring, and transferring knowledge, and at modifying its behavior to reflect new knowledge and insight” [1]. Why dedicate so much time to learning about Learning Organizations? Compared to more traditional organizations focused on deliverables and bureaucracy, learning organizations traverse changes quicker and better, remain nimble and creative in the face of challenges, and create more staff engagement. Over the course of three days, we learned about the different models and tools of a learning organization and reflected on how we could make learning and sharing knowledge a part of our workflows.

The 5 Disciplines

According to Peter Senge, whose research underpins the Learning Organization model, an organization needs 5 things (or disciplines) in order to truly learn and grow: 

  • personal mastery- individual commitment to personal growth
  • team learning- teams that learn and share together 
  • mental models- open communication where assumptions are unpacked and challenged 
  • systems thinking- an approach to projects and issues that takes a wholistic view of the organization
  • shared vision- organizational goals that can inspire everyone to move in the same direction

During the institute, we were introduced to tools for each of these areas, including strategies for having productive conversations using mental models, applying systems thinking to proactively solve problems, and a process for building a shared vision that everyone values.

Our Takeaways

Ariel: One of my biggest takeaways was the need for feedback loops that reinforce learning. On a small scale this means I am now taking five minutes at the end of the day to reflect on what went well and what I could have done differently that day. On a larger scale it means building in reflection time at the end of every project, something I currently do only in an informal way. I recently read about the Army’s After Action Review and liked the questions that they ask: What did we set out to do? What actually happened? Why did it happen? What do we do next time? [2]

Sarah:This institute really helped me organize my thoughts around organizations and what is needed to successfully move forward toward shared goals. Despite the majority of us valuing collaboration, communication, and trust, it’s not very often that we lead from a place of recognizing that shared humanness when we are trying to get our daily work done. Since attending the institute, I’ve been making more of an effort to be even more transparent with my decision-making processes and communications: checking assumptions by asking for clarification, looping in people as early as possible when new information becomes available, setting clearer expectations and boundaries, and regularly expressing gratitude for people’s efforts.  

Both of us are exploring how we can use the Learning Organization model in our projects and teams, so expect to hear more about it in the coming months!
